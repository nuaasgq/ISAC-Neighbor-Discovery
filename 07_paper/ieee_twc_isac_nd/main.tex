\title{ISAC-Assisted Topology-Aware Narrow-Beam Neighbor Discovery for Distributed-Execution UAV Swarms}

\author{Author~Names~Omitted~for~Anonymous~Review}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Narrow beams improve the link budget of millimeter-wave unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) networks, but they also turn neighbor discovery into a high-dimensional three-dimensional beam rendezvous problem.
Deterministic directional neighbor discovery protocols can provide bounded delay but are tightly coupled with network scale and scan schedules, while random protocols are flexible but suffer from long-tail discovery latency and weak topology quality within a finite discovery window.
This paper studies a distributed-execution UAV-UAV setting in which nodes share no central scheduler or undiscovered-neighbor state, while each UAV has its own navigation-frame pose and position from onboard localization.
We abstract integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) as a link-layer beam-cell occupancy prior, rather than as an oracle position estimator, and propose \method{}, an ISAC-assisted topology-aware protocol that uses sensing feedback to suppress empty beam cells, preserve exploration under sensing errors, and prioritize candidate beams for nodes with local discovered-degree deficits.
We further develop \learnedmethod{}, a black-box shared-parameter protocol-tuning variant trained at a small scale and tested for transfer to larger swarms and different beamwidths within the evaluated single-hop, finite-horizon regimes, mainly in 10--30 degree beams while treating 3--5 degree beams as stress cases.
Simulation results in the tested dynamic UAV scenarios indicate that the proposed ISAC-assisted policy reduces empty scans and improves finite-time discovery over random scanning and communication-only learned-policy baselines.
For example, in the $N=100$, 10-degree, density-scaled ten-seed baseline check, the proposed policy achieves a finite-time discovery rate of 0.3652 while the implemented communication-only baselines remain near zero under the same 600-slot horizon.
Additional robustness experiments evaluate the impact of communication-to-region range, sensing-to-communication range, false alarms, missed detections, angular-cell errors, mechanism ablations, collision-penalized discovery, and diagnostic radio-state energy accounting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Directional millimeter-wave and sub-terahertz links are attractive for UAV swarms because narrow beams can compensate for severe path loss and reduce interference.
However, narrow beams also make the first networking step, neighbor discovery (ND), substantially harder.
In a three-dimensional UAV-UAV network, two nodes can establish a link only when their transmit and receive beams satisfy a bidirectional angular alignment condition.
As the beamwidth decreases, the number of beam cells grows rapidly, and the probability of blind bidirectional alignment within a short discovery window becomes small.

Existing directional ND protocols can be broadly categorized as deterministic and random methods.
Deterministic methods design scan schedules to improve coverage and sometimes provide worst-case delay guarantees, but their performance often depends on schedule synchronization, network scale, topology assumptions, or beam-codebook structure.
Random methods require less coordination and adapt naturally to distributed deployments, but the resulting discovery process can have a long tail: some important links remain undiscovered for many slots, slowing the formation of a connected discovered-neighbor graph.
Recent UAV-specific work, such as SkyOrbs, further highlights the importance of three-dimensional directional discovery in aerial networks, but it remains primarily a directional scan-scheduling problem \cite{SkyOrbs2024TMC}.
Recent UAV ND studies also optimize delay-power tradeoffs for directional UAV networks \cite{Fan2026DelayPowerND}, highlighting that discovery latency, collision behavior, and radio activity should eventually be evaluated jointly.

Integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) provides a new cross-layer opportunity.
Rather than using communication waveforms only for packet exchange, a UAV can also obtain coarse evidence about whether a recently probed beam cell is empty or occupied.
This does not eliminate the need for link-layer handshaking: sensing is noisy, can be stale under mobility, and can be misleading in multipath channels \cite{Cui2024MultipathISAC}.
The key question addressed in this paper is therefore not physical-layer beamforming, but how a distributed link-layer ND protocol should exploit an imperfect ISAC occupancy prior.

This paper makes the following contributions.
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate distributed-execution, self-localized, neighbor-unknown UAV-UAV narrow-beam ND. Each UAV knows its own position and attitude, but before discovery it does not know neighbor identities, positions, beams, or the global topology.
  \item We abstract ISAC as an imperfect beam-cell occupancy prior with false alarms, missed detections, angular cell offsets, staleness, and finite sensing range. The prior affects action selection but never directly creates a neighbor edge.
  \item We design \method{}, an ISAC-assisted ND protocol that combines occupancy belief, exploration floors, beam-lock refinement, and local discovered-degree/topology-deficit proxies for finite-time discovered-graph formation.
  \item We develop \learnedmethod{}, a black-box shared-parameter protocol-tuning variant trained in small swarms and evaluated for transfer to larger UAV networks and different beamwidths, with the clearest current evidence in 10--30 degree beams and smoother mobility regimes.
  \item We implement a reproducible simulation workflow covering dynamic mobility, random and directional baselines, ISAC/no-ISAC comparisons, node counts from 10 to 100, and beamwidths from 3 to 30 degrees, where 10--30 degrees form the main useful operating region, 3--5 degrees are stress cases, and supplementary collision/energy-accounting probes expose remaining MAC-efficiency boundaries.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Directional Neighbor Discovery}
Early directional ND work studied probabilistic transmission and listening strategies for nodes with directional antennas \cite{Vasudevan2005DirectionalND}.
Oblivious ND later established a theoretical foundation for distributed directional discovery without prior coordination or synchronization \cite{Chen2017ObliviousND}.
Hunting-based directional ND and FastND further studied blind mmWave spatial rendezvous and accelerated 60-GHz directional discovery, but mainly as scan/beam-learning problems under communication-side assumptions \cite{Wang2017HDND,Zhou2018FastND}.
For UAV networks, SkyOrbs introduced a fast three-dimensional directional ND algorithm tailored to aerial scenarios \cite{SkyOrbs2024TMC}.
Recent work further considers delay-power optimization for UAV ND with directional antennas \cite{Fan2026DelayPowerND}.
Our work differs by treating sensing feedback as a link-layer prior and by using topology-quality proxies to guide finite-time discovery priorities.

\subsection{ISAC-Assisted Beam Management}
Side information and sensing have been used to reduce beam training overhead, from out-of-band spatial information for mmWave beam selection to sensing-assisted vehicular and UAV beam management \cite{Ali2018OutOfBand,Li2024SensingNRV2X,Cui2024SensingUAVBM}.
Radar-assisted predictive beamforming illustrates the ``communication served by sensing'' principle, while UAV mmWave/THz beam learning highlights the effect of in-flight mobility uncertainty on beam alignment \cite{Liu2020RadarPredictiveBF,Moorthy2022BeamLearningDrone}.
Recent works also study ISAC-assisted beam rendezvous in airborne networks \cite{Hong2024ISACRendezvous}, predictive beam tracking in multipath channels \cite{Cui2024MultipathISAC}, and deep-learning predictive bidirectional beamforming for ISAC-enabled UAV networks \cite{Xu2026PredictiveBidirectionalBF}.
Many of these studies focus on physical-layer or beam-management problems between a base station and mobile nodes, while airborne rendezvous studies remain closer to link establishment but still differ from distributed-execution UAV-UAV ND.
By contrast, this paper targets a distributed-execution UAV-UAV ND protocol and uses ISAC only as an imperfect occupancy-prior service exposed to the data-link layer.

\subsection{Learning for Distributed Wireless Protocols}
Multi-agent reinforcement learning and graph neural policies have been studied for wireless resource allocation and distributed channel access \cite{Naderializadeh2021MADRLTWC,Eisen2020REGNN,Guo2022MARLMAC}.
Learning has also appeared in directional ND and beam configuration \cite{Wang2024RLDirectionalND}.
This work uses shared-parameter protocol tuning to tune scalable local decision rules rather than claiming a complete neural MARL algorithm as the final contribution.
The resulting policy can be tested for transfer across swarm sizes while preserving local-observation execution.

\section{System Model}
Consider $N$ UAVs moving in a three-dimensional region.
Fig.~\ref{fig:system_model} illustrates the information boundary.
Each UAV $i$ knows its own position $\mathbf{p}_i(t)$, velocity $\mathbf{v}_i(t)$, heading, pitch, and attitude from onboard localization and inertial sensing.
Before neighbor discovery, UAV $i$ does not know the identity, position, velocity, or beam state of an undiscovered UAV $j$.
There is no central controller and no global topology knowledge.
Distributed execution here means no central scheduler and no undiscovered-neighbor state exchange; the simulator still assumes slotted operation and a common navigation reference available from onboard localization and inertial sensing.

Each UAV has a directional beam codebook $\beamset$ with $B_{\mathrm{az}}$ azimuth cells and $B_{\mathrm{el}}$ elevation cells.
The total number of three-dimensional beam cells is $|\beamset|=B_{\mathrm{az}}B_{\mathrm{el}}$.
A communication edge $(i,j)$ can be discovered in slot $t$ only if (i) the distance is within communication range $\Rc$, (ii) one endpoint transmits and the other receives, and (iii) the selected beams satisfy bidirectional angular alignment within the allowed tolerance.
Discovery is confirmed only by a successful bidirectional narrow-beam handshake.

\subsection{ISAC Prior}
When UAV $i$ probes beam $b$ in a slot, its communication waveform can also provide an ISAC observation of quantized beam-cell occupancy.
We represent this physical-layer service as a link-layer occupancy observation
\begin{equation}
  z_i^t(b) \in \{0,1\},
\end{equation}
where $z_i^t(b)=1$ indicates that beam cell $b$ is likely occupied within the sensing observation support.
This abstraction is aligned with the 3GPP view of ISAC as a sensing service that exposes sensing results with performance dimensions such as detection reliability, resolution, latency, and refreshing rate, rather than as a direct communication-link confirmation primitive \cite{ThreeGPP22137ISAC}.
For reproducibility, let $o_i^t(b)$ denote the true occupancy of beam cell $b$ within the sensing support before angular-cell error is applied.
The abstract ISAC service is generated as
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \Pr\{\tilde z_i^t(b)=1|o_i^t(b)=1\} &= 1-P_{\mathrm{md}},\\
  \Pr\{\tilde z_i^t(b)=1|o_i^t(b)=0\} &= P_{\mathrm{fa}},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:isac_detection}
\end{equation}
followed by a zero-mean discrete angular-cell offset $\Delta b\sim K_{\sigma_b}$ for positive detections, so that $z_i^t(b)=\tilde z_i^t(b-\Delta b)$ after quantization.
The kernel is truncated or wrapped at the codebook boundary in the simulator implementation.
The observation may therefore contain false alarms, missed detections, angular-cell offsets, and staleness.
The sensing range $\Rs$ denotes the support of the coarse occupancy observation, whereas $\Rc$ defines the link-confirmation range for a discoverable communication edge.
They are modeled as separate abstraction parameters because packet decoding is governed by link-budget, channel, blockage, interference, and receiver-sensitivity conditions, whereas radar-like sensing is governed by detection thresholds, waveform and aperture choices, target scattering, integration time, clutter, and medium parameters \cite{ThreeGPP38901Channel,Liu2022ISAC6G,Skolnik2001Radar,Bomfin2024SystemISAC}.
We therefore do not set $\Rs=\Rc$ by default and do not derive $\Rs$ from a specific radar equation or estimator threshold; instead, $\Rs$, false-alarm probability, missed-detection probability, angular-cell error, and sensing staleness are protocol-level input parameters that emulate the service-quality point delivered by an underlying ISAC receiver.
The observation does not reveal neighbor identity, position, velocity, or range-resolved neighbor state.
Only UAV pairs within $\Rc$ can enter the communication-neighbor set and be confirmed by handshake; therefore, $\Rs>\Rc$ may provide additional occupancy evidence but does not create immediately discoverable links in this model.

Each node maintains a local belief $q_i^t(b)$ for every beam cell.
The belief is updated from ISAC observations using a smoothing factor and decays over time to avoid over-trusting stale evidence, e.g.,
\begin{equation}
  q_i^{t+1}(b)=
  \begin{cases}
    (1-\alpha_q)q_i^t(b)+\alpha_q z_i^t(b), & b\ \mathrm{observed},\\
    (1-\delta_q)q_i^t(b)+\delta_q q_0, & b\ \mathrm{unobserved},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:belief_update}
\end{equation}
where $q_0$ is the exploration prior.
The belief only biases future beam selection and never bypasses the link-layer handshake.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{c}}^t$ denote the set of communication-range UAV pairs that exist at slot $t$, and let $\hat{\mathcal{E}}^t$ denote the set of discovered links confirmed by handshakes up to slot $t$.
Within a finite discovery horizon $T$, the protocol aims to build a useful discovered graph rather than to maximize only the number of random handshakes.
The discovered graph is a finite-horizon neighbor-knowledge graph, or discovered-edge cache, and should not be confused with a proof that the instantaneous active communication graph is connected under arbitrary later mobility.
In the main single-hop tests, $\Rc$ and the region size are chosen so that communication-range eligibility is persistent during the discovery episode; mobility-boundary tests are reported separately.
For evaluation, we use the finite-time objective
\begin{equation}
  J_T =
  w_{\mathrm{d}} \frac{|\hat{\mathcal{E}}^T|}{|\cup_{t=1}^{T}\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{c}}^t|}
  + w_{\lambda}\lambda_2(\hat{\mathcal{G}}^T)
  - w_{\mathrm{e}}\rho_{\mathrm{empty}}^T
  - w_{\mathrm{c}} C^T ,
  \label{eq:finite_objective}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathcal{G}}^T=(\mathcal{V},\hat{\mathcal{E}}^T)$, $\lambda_2(\cdot)$ is the algebraic connectivity of the discovered graph \cite{Fiedler1973AlgebraicConnectivity}, $\rho_{\mathrm{empty}}^T$ is the empty-scan ratio, and $C^T$ is the normalized collision penalty used for policy scoring.
Equation~\eqref{eq:finite_objective} is not solved by a central controller; it is an offline evaluation criterion and a design guide for local proxies.
Each UAV must choose actions from its local information history
\begin{equation}
  A_i^t \sim \pi_\theta(\mathcal{H}_i^t), \quad
  \mathcal{H}_i^t = \{\mathbf{x}_i^\tau, q_i^\tau(b), \hat{\mathcal{N}}_i^\tau\}_{\tau \le t, b\in\beamset},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_i^\tau$ is the self-state, $q_i^\tau(b)$ is the ISAC belief, and $\hat{\mathcal{N}}_i^\tau$ is the discovered-neighbor state available after confirmed handshakes.
This information constraint rules out protocols that require undiscovered-neighbor positions, global topology, or a centralized scheduler.

\section{Protocol Design}
\subsection{\method{}}
\method{} is a distributed slot-level protocol.
Its sensing-to-handshake mechanism is summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig:protocol_mechanism}.
In every slot, each UAV chooses among sensing, transmitting, receiving, and idling.
For transmission and reception, the node samples a beam according to a score that combines ISAC occupancy belief, exploration, beam diversity, staleness, and a local topology-deficit proxy.
The exploration floor prevents permanent exclusion of beam cells that were incorrectly marked empty.

For UAV $i$, the score of beam $b$ is written as
\begin{equation}
  s_i^t(b)=
  \alpha q_i^t(b)
  + \beta d_i^t(b)
  + \gamma u_i^t(b)
  + \eta r_i^t(b),
  \label{eq:beam_score}
\end{equation}
where $q_i^t(b)$ is the ISAC occupancy belief, $d_i^t(b)$ encourages beam diversity, $u_i^t(b)$ is a beam-dependent local discovered-degree/topology-deficit term, and $r_i^t(b)$ is a staleness term.
The beam sampling probability is
\begin{equation}
  P_i^t(b) =
  (1-\epsilon)\frac{\exp(\kappa s_i^t(b))}{\sum_{b'\in\beamset}\exp(\kappa s_i^t(b'))}
  + \frac{\epsilon}{|\beamset|},
  \label{eq:beam_probability}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is the exploration floor and $\kappa$ is the softmax sharpness.
Equations~\eqref{eq:beam_score} and \eqref{eq:beam_probability} make ISAC feedback useful without permanently pruning beams that may have been affected by sensing errors.

The beam-dependent topology term is implemented through the local candidate set
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{C}_i^t =
  \{b:q_i^t(b)\ge \tau_q\}
  \cup
  \{b_i^{\mathrm{lock}}:\ell_i^t>0\},
  \label{eq:candidate_set}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_q$ is the occupancy threshold, $b_i^{\mathrm{lock}}$ is a beam retained after recent positive evidence or near-miss handshakes, and $\ell_i^t$ is its remaining lock lifetime.
The discovered-degree/topology-deficit proxy is
\begin{equation}
  u_i^t(b)=
  \mathbb{1}\{b\in\mathcal{C}_i^t\}
  \max\{0,d_{\mathrm{tar}}-|\hat{\mathcal{N}}_i^t|\},
  \label{eq:topology_value}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\mathrm{tar}}$ is the target discovered degree.
Thus topology need affects beam selection only for beams with current occupancy or lock evidence, and does not require undiscovered-neighbor identities or global topology.
Within the candidate set, this term is a degree-deficit gate rather than a marginal $\lambda_2$ estimator for each individual beam.

The local proxy gives larger priority to candidate beams that may establish links for nodes whose discovered degree is below a target degree.
This is not an online computation of global algebraic connectivity.
It is a local rule designed to encourage finite-time formation of a well-connected discovered graph.
We use $\lambda_2$ as a topology-quality proxy related to consensus performance in networked agents \cite{OlfatiSaber2004Consensus}, but this paper does not prove a closed-loop consensus-control convergence theorem.

At the end of each slot, node $i$ updates $q_i^{t+1}(b)$ from the current ISAC observation, aging previously unobserved cells toward an exploration prior.
If $z_i^t(b)=1$ or a handshake attempt through $b$ produces near-miss evidence, the node inserts $b$ into $\mathcal{C}_i^{t+1}$ and sets $\ell_i^{t+1}$ to a finite lock lifetime; otherwise the lock lifetime decays by one slot.
A confirmed bidirectional handshake adds the neighbor to $\hat{\mathcal{N}}_i^{t+1}$.
The one-slot delayed ablation uses the same updates but applies $\mathcal{C}_i^{t}$ only from the next slot, bounding the low-latency same-slot implementation assumption.

\subsection{Shared-Parameter Protocol Tuning}
\learnedmethod{} uses the same information boundary as \method{} but tunes the decision weights with a shared policy.
Fig.~\ref{fig:policy_architecture} shows the shared-policy execution structure.
The implemented training loop uses cross-entropy style black-box search over distributed protocol parameters.
This design keeps deployment distributed at execution time: all UAVs reuse the same parameters and act only on their local histories.
Neural actor-critic and value-decomposition variants can be built on the same environment, but they are not used as the main performance evidence in this paper.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\subsection{Mobility and Slot Timing}
The simulation uses dynamic UAV mobility, including Gauss-Markov, random-walk, and random-direction/waypoint variants.
The slot duration is set to 5 ms so that ISAC observations remain meaningful within the mobility timescale.
The primary training configuration uses $N=10$ UAVs and 10-degree beams, and evaluates transfer to larger swarms. The 5 ms slot duration is an explicit modeling assumption rather than a physical-layer timing result.
Unless otherwise noted, the multi-seed large-scale tables report means over three independent random seeds; standard-deviation and confidence-interval summaries are kept in supplementary reproducibility tables.

\subsection{Baselines}
The comparison set includes uniform random scanning, a SkyOrbs-like directional baseline, a learned communication-only shared policy, an enhanced communication-only shared policy, and the proposed enhanced ISAC-assisted shared policy.
The SkyOrbs-like baseline implements a deterministic three-dimensional skip-scan schedule inspired by SkyOrbs, but it is not a strict reproduction of the complete SkyOrbs protocol.
The two no-ISAC learning baselines disable sensing-derived occupancy priors, candidate masks, and beam-lock updates at execution; the enhanced no-ISAC variant keeps the corresponding non-sensing policy refinements so that the comparison isolates the value of the ISAC observation stream.
Mechanism ablations remove candidate-set refinement, beam-lock refinement, or topology-deficit scoring from the proposed protocol.

\subsection{Metrics}
We report finite-time discovery rate, discovered edge count, mean discovery delay, empty-scan ratio, collision count, largest connected component ratio, and algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ of the discovered graph.
Because high-percentile discovery delays can be censored by a finite simulation horizon, the main claims emphasize discovery rate, empty-scan ratio, edge count, and connectivity metrics.
We also report collision-penalized discovery in supplementary dense-regime analyses and assumed radio-state accounting as diagnostic evidence; these metrics are not presented as a platform-calibrated energy-optimality result.
For range sweeps, $D$ denotes the region diagonal.

\subsection{Main Transfer Results}
The multi-seed transfer results indicate that the enhanced ISAC-assisted shared policy transfers without fine-tuning from $N=10$ and 10-degree beams to $N=100$ in the evaluated single-hop, 600-slot regimes, with useful behavior mainly for 10--30 degree beams and smoother mobility.
For example, in the single-hop density-scaled setting at $N=100$ and 10-degree beams, the cumulative discovered-neighbor graph becomes connected within the finite horizon, although the finite-time discovery rate is far from complete.
This supports the claim that ISAC priors help form a useful connected discovered-neighbor graph, but it does not imply that extremely narrow beams are solved.
This trend is consistent across the $N=100$ multi-seed checks, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:n100_transfer}; a separate broad stress sweep includes 3-degree beams and confirms that this extremely narrow setting remains outside the useful operating region under the current horizon.
Under density-preserving scaling, the proposed policy reaches discovery rates of 0.3655, 0.5440, and 0.4568 for 10-, 15-, and 30-degree beams, respectively, while the 5-degree case remains much weaker at 0.0814.
The 30-degree setting reduces empty scans but also creates many more collision attempts; therefore, raw discovery and connectivity favor a 15-degree operating point in the current tests, whereas collision-aware efficiency favors narrower 10-degree beams.
Fixed-area scaling gives a similar trend, indicating that the conclusion is not an artifact of one area-scaling convention.
Figs.~\ref{fig:n100_transfer_density} and \ref{fig:n100_area_scale} visualize these transfer trends.
Table~\ref{tab:n100_baseline} further isolates the main comparison at $N=100$ and 10-degree beams.
In this narrow-beam large-swarm setting, the implemented communication-only baselines, including a deterministic SkyOrbs-like 3-D skip-scan reference that should not be interpreted as a full SkyOrbs reproduction, spend most trials on empty cells under our information boundary and simulation assumptions.
The ISAC-assisted policy forms a connected discovered-neighbor graph with a finite-time discovery rate of 0.3652 in the same finite horizon.
A strict head-to-head comparison with the complete SkyOrbs protocol is left as future work because SkyOrbs uses protocol assumptions and scan-schedule details that are not fully reproduced here.

\subsection{Range, Error, and Mechanism Robustness}
The robustness experiments evaluate three implementation-critical checks:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Rc/D$ and $\Rs/\Rc$ sensitivity, where $D$ is the region diagonal.
  \item ISAC false-alarm, missed-detection, and angular-cell-offset robustness.
  \item Mechanism ablations for candidate-set refinement, beam-lock refinement, topology-deficit scoring, and implementation-delay sensitivity.
\end{itemize}
These results are used to separate supported robustness claims from remaining implementation and physical-layer caveats.
Within this communication-neighbor-discovery objective, the marginal protocol gain saturates once $\Rs$ approaches $\Rc$ because only communication-range neighbors are eligible for immediate handshake confirmation.
This saturation is a consequence of the modeled link-confirmation objective, not a statement that sensing range cannot physically exceed communication range.
At $\Rc/D=0.65$, increasing $\Rs/\Rc$ from 0.5 to 0.75 improves the proposed policy's discovery rate from 0.2628 to 0.3569.
Increasing $\Rs$ beyond $\Rc$ does not provide additional gain in the tested sweep and finite-horizon link-confirmation objective because only communication-range neighbors can be immediately confirmed by handshake.
The slot-duration sweep further indicates that the 5 ms slot assumption is not a tuned single point: at N=100 and 10-degree beams, the proposed policy's discovery rate remains between 0.3564 and 0.3696 when the slot duration varies from 1 ms to 20 ms under Gauss-Markov mobility.
Fig.~\ref{fig:range_gain_heatmap} summarizes the range sensitivity.

The sensing-error experiments indicate a bounded degradation rather than a collapse.
In the paired 600-slot profile sweep, the proposed policy's discovery rate decreases from 0.3655 under ideal sensing to 0.3357 under $(P_{\mathrm{fa}},P_{\mathrm{md}},\sigma_b)=(0.01,0.05,0.5)$ and to 0.2854 under $(0.05,0.15,1.0)$.
The severe $(0.10,0.30,1.5)$ profile remains around 0.2935 in this stochastic run.
These results support the weaker but important claim that the ISAC prior remains useful under moderate sensing errors; they do not imply immunity to severe sensing faults.
At 15-degree beams, supplementary checks show the same distinction between favorable and boundary regimes: Gauss-Markov discovery remains between 0.5272 and 0.5852 across the configured error profiles, whereas random-walk discovery decreases from 0.4414 to 0.2253 as error severity increases.

In the $N=100$, 10-degree ablation, candidate-set refinement causes the largest observed performance loss when removed, suggesting that ISAC-assisted candidate formation is the dominant mechanism for the main operating point rather than a universal claim across all beamwidths and mobility models.
At $N=100$ and 10-degree beams, removing the ISAC-driven candidate-set refinement reduces the discovery rate from 0.3655 to 0.0313 and drives the discovered graph connectivity to nearly zero.
Removing topology-deficit scoring or beam-lock refinement causes a smaller discovery-rate degradation in this setting.
This result is also a modeling caveat: the low-latency candidate-set refinement allows ISAC-updated candidate beams to affect readiness inside the discovery opportunity represented by a slot.
To bound this assumption, we evaluate a one-slot delayed candidate-set variant.
The delayed variant obtains a discovery rate of 0.2989 and $\lambda_2=8.4709$, compared with 0.3655 and 12.9222 for the low-latency variant and 0.0007 for the no-ISAC learning baseline.
It also reduces collisions from 1050.0 to 697.0, and its collision-penalized discovery rate remains 0.2620 versus 0.3015 for the low-latency variant.
This supports interpreting the proposed protocol as a low-latency design point and the delayed variant as a practical implementation boundary.
This B=10 result should not be read as collision optimality: the supplementary paired B=15 checks show that one-slot delay can be more collision-efficient, while a ten-seed local role-control probe can mitigate that boundary, making collision-aware MAC refinement a separate protocol-layer problem.

\section{Limitations}
This study has several important limitations.
First, the learning component is a shared-parameter protocol-tuning workflow, not yet a full MAPPO, QMIX, or graph neural MARL implementation.
Second, the SkyOrbs-like comparison is an inspired baseline rather than a strict reproduction of the complete original algorithm.
Third, ISAC is represented by an abstract beam-cell occupancy prior; physical waveform design and estimator implementation are outside the scope.
Fourth, candidate-set refinement abstracts sub-slot or readiness behavior; the one-slot delayed candidate-set ablation bounds this implementation assumption but does not replace a hardware-specific timing study.
Fifth, extremely narrow beams at 3 and 5 degrees and abrupt mobility models remain stress regions and should be discussed as such.
Sixth, the supplement includes assumed radio-state energy accounting, but calibrated delay-power optimization would require platform-specific power states and traffic models.
Finally, the proposed ISAC-assisted policy creates more collision attempts than the no-ISAC baseline in dense finite-horizon tests.
Therefore, collision-penalized discovery should be interpreted together with raw discovery rate in dense finite-horizon settings.
A supplementary collision-aware role-control probe mitigates this boundary in the tested B=10/B=15 paired settings, but a complete collision- and energy-aware MAC remains outside the present scope.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper studies ISAC-assisted narrow-beam neighbor discovery for distributed-execution UAV swarms.
The central idea is to use ISAC not as an oracle, but as an imperfect beam-cell prior that reduces empty scanning in the evaluated regimes while preserving link-layer handshake confirmation.
The simulation workflow supports the feasibility of this direction in the evaluated single-hop, finite-horizon regimes and provides transfer, range, sensing-error, and implementation-boundary evidence for a cross-layer ISAC-assisted neighbor-discovery protocol.
The results also identify clear boundaries: extremely narrow 3--5 degree beams, abrupt mobility, strict physical-layer ISAC realization, and collision-aware efficiency remain open issues rather than solved cases.
