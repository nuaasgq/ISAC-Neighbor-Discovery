\section{Scope of This Supplement}

This supplement records the experiment coverage and stress evidence supporting the bounded claims in the main manuscript.
The main paper should be read as a cross-layer link-layer neighbor-discovery study: ISAC provides an imperfect beam-cell occupancy prior, and neighbor edges are created only by bidirectional narrow-beam handshakes.
The additional figures and tables below are used to bound the claims rather than to assert universal robustness.

At each 5 ms discovery slot, a UAV ages local belief/lock timers, selects a local radio role, samples a beam from the main-paper score if transmitting or receiving, attempts a narrow-beam handshake, and adds a discovered edge only after bidirectional alignment within communication range.
The ISAC observation then updates the local beam-cell belief: empty observations suppress later visits, while occupied observations or near-miss evidence can refresh a candidate lock.
The round13 collision-aware refinement changes only local role probabilities under candidate and collision pressure; it does not change the ISAC candidate-set interface or the handshake-confirmation rule.
Thus sensing is a priority signal for future link-layer actions rather than a direct edge-creation event.

\section{Baseline Scope Note}

The SkyOrbs-like skip-scan baseline is included as a deterministic three-dimensional directional scan-scheduling reference under the same finite-horizon simulator information boundary as the other communication-only baselines.
It uses the same beam codebook, mobility trace, range setting, slot horizon, and evaluation metrics as the proposed protocol, but disables ISAC-derived occupancy, candidate, and beam-lock observations.
It should not be interpreted as a strict reproduction of the complete SkyOrbs protocol: the original beam-indexing order, scan-cycle synchronization, antenna switching, motion assumptions, and published-trend reproduction have not all been reconstructed here.
Accordingly, the main claim is not a head-to-head claim against complete SkyOrbs; the SkyOrbs-like results are used to contextualize deterministic communication-only scan scheduling, while the core evidence comes from no-ISAC controls and ISAC mechanism ablations.

\subsection{Collision-Aware MAC Refinement Probe}

To probe whether part of the B=15 collision-penalized boundary can be reduced by local role selection, we add a lightweight distributed variant, \texttt{collision\_aware\_isac}.
It keeps the same ISAC candidate-set interface and handshake readiness rule as the proposed protocol, but stores a beam-level collision counter when multiple transmitters address the same receiver and lowers the local TX probability under candidate and collision pressure.
In a ten-seed extension of the same paired setting, the collision-aware variant obtains B=10 discovery 0.3660 and collision-penalized discovery 0.3147, compared with 0.3652 and 0.2991 for the proposed low-latency variant.
At B=15, it obtains discovery 0.5647 and collision-penalized discovery 0.2479, compared with 0.5421 and 0.2017 for the proposed low-latency variant and 0.5107 and 0.2209 for the one-slot delayed variant.
The collision-penalized delta is positive in all 10/10 paired seeds versus both the proposed low-latency protocol and the one-slot delayed variant for B=10 and B=15.
This probe does not close the full MAC-design problem; it shows that the collision boundary identified in Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_round11_stability} is mitigated in this ten-seed paired setting by distributed role-control refinement.

\section{Learned-Component Ablation of the Phase10 Actor}

To separate learned actor weights from the structured ISAC candidate interface and rule residuals, we add a focused evaluation-time ablation at $N=100$, 10-degree beams, and 3000 slots.
All variants use the same decentralized observation boundary and the same final evaluation setting; the ablation changes only the loaded policy weights or the selected structured feature path.
This probe is deliberately interpreted as component evidence rather than as a replacement for the multi-seed transfer table.

Table~\ref{tab:supp_learned_ablation} and Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_learned_ablation} show that random-weight and zero-weight/rule-only policies can produce higher raw discovery and $\lambda_2$ because they access the channel aggressively.
That behavior is not the same as a better protocol: their collision counts increase by factors of 5.4 and 6.7 relative to the trained full actor.
The trained actor reduces mean collisions by 81.4\% relative to random weights and by 85.2\% relative to the zero-weight/rule-only control, yielding a higher collision-penalized discovery rate than both controls.
Removing the rule residual lowers collision count but also lowers raw discovery and $\lambda_2$.
Disabling the hard candidate mask gives a high CPD value in this three-episode probe, but its empty-scan ratio rises to 0.8173, so it is treated as a boundary signal for mask design rather than as a ready replacement for the final actor.
The defensible conclusion is therefore narrow: learned weights help shape the collision-efficiency operating point under strong ISAC/rule priors, while the candidate mask and residual priors define the broader discovery/collision/empty-scan tradeoff.

\section{Historical Structured Neural MARL Probe}

Before the current slot-level MARL evidence line, we implemented a smaller structured shared actor-critic probe to test whether the ISAC candidate-set mechanism could be exposed to a neural policy without violating decentralized execution.
The actor observes only local belief, beam memory, candidate masks, candidate scores, local discovered-degree deficit, and rule-prior logits.
The centralized truth interface remains separate and is not used by the actor.
This historical probe is retained for traceability only and is superseded by the Phase10 contention-aware and gate-family MARL results used in the main manuscript.

In a N=10, B=72, 80-slot behavior-cloning probe with three training seeds and five held-out evaluation episodes per seed, stochastic evaluation is nonzero for all evaluated variants.
The flat stochastic actor reaches a mean discovery rate of 0.632, while the full candidate-score/topology/rule-residual actor reaches 0.557.
Thus the structured neural probe does not yet outperform the flat stochastic student.
However, the full structured actor improves deterministic execution from 0.0015 discovery for the flat actor to 0.0643 and makes 14 out of 15 deterministic evaluations nonzero.
It also reduces stochastic empty-scan ratio from 0.690 for the flat actor to 0.111, at the cost of unresolved collision coordination.
This supports using the structured actor as a feasible neural extension, not as the current main method.
A short 10-episode actor-critic fine-tuning follow-up increases the full structured stochastic discovery rate to 0.586 and leaves deterministic discovery near 0.066, but it still does not overtake the flat stochastic student.
A residual-strength sweep identifies $\lambda_{\mathrm{rule}}=0.25$ as a better balanced setting in this probe, with stochastic discovery 0.598 and deterministic discovery 0.084 with all 15 deterministic evaluations nonzero.
A clean no-ISAC neural baseline, with both the environment and expert protocol set to the no-ISAC variant, reduces deterministic discovery to 0 and stochastic discovery to 0.0044.
This indicates that the neural probe's nonzero behavior is driven by the ISAC-enabled local candidate observations rather than by blind stochastic alignment alone.
A label-only no-ISAC follow-up is treated only as a label-sensitivity sanity check because its evaluation environment still exposes ISAC candidate observations.
