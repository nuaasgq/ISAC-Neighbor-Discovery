\section{Scope of This Supplement}

This supplement records the experiment coverage and stress evidence supporting the bounded claims in the main manuscript.
The main paper should be read as a cross-layer link-layer neighbor-discovery study: ISAC provides an imperfect beam-cell occupancy prior, and neighbor edges are created only by bidirectional narrow-beam handshakes.
The additional figures and tables below are used to bound the claims rather than to assert universal robustness.

\subsection{Collision-Aware MAC Refinement Probe}

To probe whether part of the B=15 collision-penalized boundary can be reduced by local role selection, we add a lightweight distributed variant, \texttt{collision\_aware\_isac}.
It keeps the same ISAC candidate-set interface and handshake readiness rule as the proposed protocol, but stores a beam-level collision counter when multiple transmitters address the same receiver and lowers the local TX probability under candidate and collision pressure.
In a ten-seed extension of the same paired setting, the collision-aware variant obtains B=10 discovery 0.3660 and collision-penalized discovery 0.3147, compared with 0.3652 and 0.2991 for the proposed low-latency variant.
At B=15, it obtains discovery 0.5647 and collision-penalized discovery 0.2479, compared with 0.5421 and 0.2017 for the proposed low-latency variant and 0.5107 and 0.2209 for the one-slot delayed variant.
The collision-penalized delta is positive in all 10/10 paired seeds versus both the proposed low-latency protocol and the one-slot delayed variant for B=10 and B=15.
This probe does not close the full MAC-design problem; it shows that the collision boundary identified in Fig.~\ref{fig:supp_round11_stability} is mitigated in this ten-seed paired setting by distributed role-control refinement.

\section{Structured Neural MARL Probe}

The main manuscript intentionally does not claim a complete neural MARL method.
Nevertheless, we implemented a structured shared actor-critic probe to test whether the ISAC candidate-set mechanism can be exposed to a neural policy without violating decentralized execution.
The actor observes only local belief, beam memory, candidate masks, candidate scores, local discovered-degree deficit, and rule-prior logits.
The centralized truth interface remains separate and is not used by the actor.

In a N=10, B=72, 80-slot behavior-cloning probe with three training seeds and five held-out evaluation episodes per seed, stochastic evaluation is nonzero for all evaluated variants.
The flat stochastic actor reaches a mean discovery rate of 0.632, while the full candidate-score/topology/rule-residual actor reaches 0.557.
Thus the structured neural probe does not yet outperform the flat stochastic student.
However, the full structured actor improves deterministic execution from 0.0015 discovery for the flat actor to 0.0643 and makes 14 out of 15 deterministic evaluations nonzero.
It also reduces stochastic empty-scan ratio from 0.690 for the flat actor to 0.111, at the cost of unresolved collision coordination.
This supports using the structured actor as a feasible neural extension, not as the current main method.
A short 10-episode actor-critic fine-tuning follow-up increases the full structured stochastic discovery rate to 0.586 and leaves deterministic discovery near 0.066, but it still does not overtake the flat stochastic student.
A residual-strength sweep identifies $\lambda_{\mathrm{rule}}=0.25$ as a better balanced setting in this probe, with stochastic discovery 0.598 and deterministic discovery 0.084 with all 15 deterministic evaluations nonzero.
A clean no-ISAC neural baseline, with both the environment and expert protocol set to the no-ISAC variant, reduces deterministic discovery to 0 and stochastic discovery to 0.0044.
This indicates that the neural probe's nonzero behavior is driven by the ISAC-enabled local candidate observations rather than by blind stochastic alignment alone.
A label-only no-ISAC follow-up is treated only as a label-sensitivity sanity check because its evaluation environment still exposes ISAC candidate observations.
